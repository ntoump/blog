Breakthrough #1 - Batch Size

The basic concept regarding choosing a batch size is composed of two contrasting ideas:

  • the more data you can fit in a training batch, the better represented the whole of the dataset in the calculated derivative is. That would suggest that the Stochastic Gradient Descent step would be, theoretically, perfectly representative of the specific dataset,
  • on the other hand, the less data you fit in a batch, the better represented segments of the dataset are, which can lead the process in gradual excellence.

There are obvious problems in each idea. That's why new practitioners are taught to segment the ds into multiple batches (never only one), albeit their batches should be big enough to reap the benefits of that size, so long as their VRAM can support this increase.

Is bigger batch size better?

With the abovementioned mindset, I experimented with increasing the bs, as I used to do in other DL projects, from 64 up to 256, all the while trying usual intermediate values (e.g. 128).

To my great surprise, increasing the bs not only did not improve the overall accuracy, but it actively hurt it badly. In my personal notes this persistent across many models observation is phrased as follows: "[by increasing the bs to 256, the accuracy] dipped to 60% (out of ~79% with bs=64) and it would not increase no matter what (it had, impressively and effectively, been stuck at this level)".

How about smaller then?

Model with bs = 32

Firstly, I tried the usual suspect, bs=32.

Training

It seemed to be on par with the bs=64 models. That was up until I turned up the volume;

Review

We see that the accuracy climbed immediately up to 83-84%, effectively surpassing the old record. The unexplicable (at least by me and for now) is that it seemed to keep on improving after more epochs of training, even though it is blatantly overfitting to the training ds (as the trend of the validation loss suggests). It reached, finally, a peak of 86.5% accuracy after some 18 epochs of training.

Model with bs = 16

I decided to keep on decreasing the bs, up until it was obvious that there isn't anymore room for improvement.

Training
Review

The model reached, this time, a peak of 87.65% accuracy after 18 epochs. Training took more (literally, double the) time, as there were double the batches to go through each epoch.

Second Submission

After trying out many (un)usual smaller values, I found that the sweet spot for this problem is ~14-16 items per batch. The results seemed to consistently peak at around 87%-88% accuracy.

After that, I followed a series of steps that must be completed successfully in order to sumbit to Kaggle. Specifically, I had to:

  • construct a decent model, based on my recent findings,
  • complete its training routine before it starts overfitting,
  • perform inference on the testing dataset (in which there are no dependent variable columns, as that's how the model's submissions are evaluated by Kaggle),
  • submit the predictions and get the new score.

Long story short...

Evaluation

This performance leap really took me by surprise. We see an increase of almost 13% accuracy (from 64% to ~77%)! I cannot, thereby, but admit that this approach had a significant effect on my Scoreboard ranking (I think this entry put me in the top 10%).
